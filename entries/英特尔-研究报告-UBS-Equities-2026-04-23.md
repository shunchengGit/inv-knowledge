Summary 
Guidance was better than most pre-call bogeys, and the narrative on manufacturing and 
foundry continues to move in the right direction (consistent w/our commentary for well 
over a year now - here, here, here, here, here). The rapid inflection in server CPU demand 
has put INTC in a position to better control its destiny this year so the odds are that even 
the implied commentary for 2H:26 is still probably conservative and numbers still have 
upside. As we have said last Q and prior, the newsflow on foundry should also remain 
positive as the release of the 14A production design kit (PDK 1.0) late this year probably 
catalyzes one or more foundry deals with NVDA, AAPL (M-Series), maybe Amazon 
(maybe for back-end) and a high-end consumer product. As for Terafab, this is a splashy 
headline, but as we posed our Q to management on the earnings call, we think this is 
likely to be more of a technology licensing deal than a traditional foundry volume deal - 
so it is a validation of the process technology, but may not really help the 3rd party 
foundry revenue per se. The manufacturing narrative is playing out about as we 
expected, but we have under-estimated how much the market was willing to overlook a 
lack of earnings power. This manufacturing narrative should continue to improve and 
INTC will likely sign a number of new foundry deals, but at this price (up ~15% 
aftermarket and now having essentially 2x in the past month), we find it hard to create a 
compelling valuation upside case. Even if we account for a mega bull case, it is quite 
hard to get something in the $5 range and even this is several years out making it hard to 
see a ton of upside from ~$80 in the aftermarket though we certainly acknowledge that 
the foundry story will keep getting better. Net, we raise estimates with 
C2026/2027/2028 EPS going from $0.44/$0.75/$1.05 to $1.39/$1.95/$2.50 and our PT 
moves to $83 but we maintain Neutral.

INTC suggested that it undershipped demand by at least $1 billion and in light of 
flat 2H:26 expectations for the CCG revenues, our interpretation is that it is largely 
related to the DCAI which suggests demand is ~20% above the MarQ shipments - 
we see a very strong indicator for the DCAI market going forward. We model 
DCAI revenues growing 42% Y/Y in C2026 and nearly 30% in C2027. At the 
same time we expect CCG to be roughly flat this year and decline 7% in C2027.

The manufacturing/foundry message was very positive with 18A reaching target 
yields ~6mos faster than plan and a more full throated message that it will sign 
foundry deals in 2H:26-1H:27 (as we have said here, here, here, here, here) once 
customers receive the production PDK for 14A. When queried about Terafab, the 
company had little new to say other than this being part of the broader foundry 
strategy though we believe this is likely going to be a different model from the 
traditional foundry model. 

For DCAI, if we flow through the rest of the year given the guidance, it implies 
C2026 revenue growth of nearly 40% for the year - with 3Q and 4Q growing at 
8-9% Q/Q. When queried, INTC indicated that part of this is due to price increases 
having run their course in the CQ2 guidance - again this all seems conservative to 
us and certainly suggests there could be a lot of upside to our AMD server CPU 
revenue growth of ~45% as AMD will likely significantly outgrow INTC this year. 

FCF was far below at a loss of $2B, driven by working capital of fully -$3.5B, almost 
$5B worse than UBSe and Street +$1.2B, as well as gross capex of $5B ~$500MM/
$1B above UBSe/Street. Full year capex will be flat vs prior commentary that capex 
would be flat to down, but within this shell spending is well down and WFE is up 
~25% and the commentary on C2027 was also quite bullish with the company 
indicating that because SPE suppliers are booked out this year, it could not really 
raise capex even if it wanted to do so. We model Gross Capex increasing from 
$18B in C2026 to $21-22B in C2027, but anticipate governmental subsidies to 
step down Y/Y. We do note that it is not unusual for foundry customers to enter in 
prepayment for the capacity which we would expect to ultimately support INTC's 
cash flow as capex ramps.

INTC inventories have increased from $11.6B in 4Q to $12.4B in 1Q as the increase 
in WIP inventories (up $1.2B Q/Q) was partially offset by the slight decline in 
finished goods. Finished good inventories at 28 days are notably below ~44 days 
level a year ago.

NCI was $553M, $200-300MM heavy vs UBSe $325MM/Street $258MM, but 
should step down sequentially to $250MM in 2Q-4Q given the Apollo SCIP exit on 
April 1st. Full year NCI should be ~$1.1B in 2027 and 2028, consistent with the 
current 2Q+ pace post Apollo, despite continuing to tool out Fab 52 in Ocotillo, 
AZ. For NCI, we originally expected Apollo to reduce NCI by less than what now 
appears to be the case (because it is a smaller deal than the Brookfield SCIP). 
However, the company is now clarifying that Brookfield (SCIP2) is lease accounting 
and even though it is a larger deal, it actually has slightly less negative impact to 
the P&L than SCIP1 with Apollo (thus why INTC chose to take Apollo out first). We 
continue to believe that INTC will find a way to also take out the Brookfield SCIP, 
potentially using some of the foundry pre-pays or maybe via some low cost loans 
that we think are still available through the prior CHIPS Act program.

Valuation: PT 83 (from $65)
We raise our PT from $65 to $83 based on our SOTP valuation framework that values 
each business separately. We value INTC's product business on a 23x EV/EBIT multiple 
(average of the broad compute peer set) from prior 19x. Our framework looks at an 
independent valuation for Intel's manufacturing assets based on how other global 
foundry comps are valued per installed wafer of capacity. We value INTC's wafer 
capacity using a higher EV per capacity of $232 (vs. prior $220) based on the same 
group of peer foundries as well as TSMC (applying a 45% haircut to reflect node and 
yield gaps, customer stickiness, and utilization risk), and netting off the non-controlling 
interest (basically debt represented by the SCIP agreements for Arizona and Ireland). See 
Valuation and Multiple Justification.

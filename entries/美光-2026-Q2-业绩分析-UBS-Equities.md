FQ3:26 (May) Earnings Preview: LTAs  + Agentic 
AI as Key Elements to the Re-Rating Story
Summary Thoughts
Pricing continues to move higher, consistent with our latest round of industry checks and  
now reflected in our updated MU model. Consequently, we remain well ahead of MU’s 
May Q outlook and Street -  with management's commentary at its most recent 
conference appearance implying the Q tracked above guidance - and we see a strong  
Aug Q guide setup. Against this backdrop, we expect the call to center around LTAs (our 
team’s work here and here) and agentic AI. On LTAs specifically, we think MU is likely to 
point to multiple agreements, versus a single 'SCA' last quarter, and may hint at the 
structure or hooks of these arrangements. Recall our expectation that up to 30% of 
industry-wide DDR volumes  will soon be locked in at pricing that is just slightly below 
current levels. Equally important, we expect MU to reinforce how agentic AI is reshaping 
memory demand, with KV cache growth increasingly the  key system-level bottleneck 
and hardware differentiator (our notes from the agentic AI teach-in with MU at the back 
of this note). Net, we are again raising C2026-2029E EPS modestly, with estimates still 
comfortably >$100 throughout the period - and continue to believe it is only a matter of 
time before the market assigns MU a more normal multiple as details emerge around 
the structural changes AI has driven across the entire memory complex. We leave our 
$1,625 price target unchanged, based on ~15x NTM P/E applied to our C2029E EPS of 
$125, discounted back 1-yr, and reiterate our Buy rating.
Expect Solid Quarter/Guidance
We model FQ3:26 (May) revenue/EPS $36.0B/$20.96, well ahead of MU's guidance with 
upside practically all driven by better pricing - consistent with our  industry checks and 
management's commentary throughout the quarter. Within DRAM, we forecast 
revenue of $28.1B (bits +3% Q/Q, ASP +45% Q/Q) and within NAND, revenue of $7.9B 
(bits +5% Q/Q, ASP +50% Q/Q). For HBM specifically, we model revenue of $2.9B (bits 
+13% Q/Q, ASP +2% Q/Q) while for Core DRAM (ex-HBM) we forecast revenue of 
$25.2B (bits +2% Q/Q, ASP +51% Q/Q). On gross margin, we stand ~210bps Q/Q 
ahead of the May Q guidance of ~81% and model a +230bps sequential increase in Aug 
Q to 85.4%. We now see gross margin peaking in the ~90% range across C3Q27E/
C4Q27E, and leading to overall MU gross margin of 83.7%/89.4%/88.7% in 
C2026/2027/2028E. Looking ahead to FQ4:26 (Aug), we expect pricing strength to 
persist and model revenue/EPS of $43.1B/$25.64, with DRAM revenue of $33.4B (bits 
+3% Q/Q, ASP +15% Q/Q) and NAND revenue of $9.6B (bits +6% Q/Q and ASP +15% 
Q/Q). Given the robust demand backdrop and strengthening profitability, we model 
gross capex of $31B/$42B/$41.5B in C2026/2027/2028 (inclusive of ~$5B/yr in 
government incentives across the out-years).

Valuation: PT $1,625 (unchanged)
Our price target of  $1,625 stays  unchanged. This is based on a ~15x NTM P/E multiple 
(noting we see no reason why MU should trade a whole lot differently than other semi 
companies in terms of P/E), which is in line with the 3-yr average, and applied to our ~
$124 C2029E EPS and discounted back to C2028E using a COE of ~12%. Importantly, 
we anchor on C2029E EPS as we believe it best reflects MU’s through-cycle earnings 
power under LTAs because, by that point, our model assumes a moderate memory 
downcycle, albeit with LTAs buoying the company’s earnings base.
Insights from the MU Teach-In on Agentic AI
Below, we summarize our  key takeaways from a recent investor call hosted  with MU 
management to discuss the implications of agentic AI on memory:

Agentic AI drives a step-function increase in memory intensity, as query 
context grows ~30x/yr. MU flagged that, different from traditional “single-
shot” inference today, the workflow behind a single agentic prompt can spawn 
~4-5 simultaneous agents, each executing separate CPU-based tasks while 
continuously interfacing with GPU/ASIC accelerators. Consequently, this creates a 
multiplicative increase in hardware utilization across CPU cycles, DRAM capacity, 
GPU time, HBM bandwidth, and SSD storage - all of which imply materially higher 
memory content per AI query versus prior (non-agentic) inference architectures.

HBM remains central as the critical enabler of agentic AI. MU emphasized 
that active KV cache workloads require extremely high bandwidth memory sitting 
directly adjacent to accelerators, with HBM serving as the “intelligence layer” of 
the system - as how useful agents are effectively comes down to their 
"intelligence" which is dictated by the LLM, and therefore the processing done on 
the GPU, where HBM is still key. Notably, MU quantified current HBM-resident KV 
cache at roughly ~10–100GB per GPU, with bandwidth requirements ranging 
from ~1–20TB/s and data residency measured in milliseconds to seconds - all of 
this reinforces the view that AI scaling increasingly depends on memory 
bandwidth and not simply accelerator FLOPS.

With agentic AI, the entirety of the memory hierarchy stack becoming 
increasingly important. Beyond HBM, MU suggested the next memory tier 
could support roughly ~200-400GB of KV cache per GPU using DRAM, with lower 
bandwidth (~100-500GB/s) and residency times measured in seconds to minutes. 
Micron highlighted its new 192GB SOCAMM products already shipping in volume, 
alongside newly announced 256GB SOCAMM and 256GB RDIMM products, 
which management said can drive roughly ~3x higher DRAM capacity per CPU 
socket versus prior generation systems. Further down the stack, management 
discussed pooled/disaggregated memory architectures potentially scaling into 
terabytes of DRAM capacity per node.

SSDs increasingly strategic as KV cache and persistent-context scale 
beyond DRAM. MU described NAND as a new “context memory storage” tier 
capable of supporting tens-to-hundreds of terabytes of KV cache, particularly for 
warm data and vector-database workloads. The company highlighted its newly 
announced 245TB SSD and noted that a rack-level deployment could therefore 
support multi-petabyte-scale AI storage architectures. Importantly, MU suggested 
SSD attach rates within DC infrastructure should continue to increase structurally 
as AI workloads shift more data into “warmer” environments (and away from cold 
storage).

Future SW optimizations (like TurboQuant) set to ultimately expand 
memory consumption. The company acknowledged ongoing advances in 
quantization, sparse attention, and inference optimization, but argued the 
industry is more likely to reinvest those efficiency gains into larger context 
windows, more concurrent agents, and higher reasoning complexity rather than 
reducing hardware content. MU pointed specifically to the multiplicative nature of 
agentic AI (as one query spawns multiple agents) as evidence that lower inference 
costs could accelerate overall infrastructure demand growth rather than 
suppressing HBM, DRAM, or NAND consumption.


bandwidth-memory/agentic-ai-in-data-centers-and-role-of-hbm-white-paper.pdf

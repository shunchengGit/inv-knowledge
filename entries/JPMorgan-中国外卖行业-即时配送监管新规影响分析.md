We believe the market may misread SAMR’s 17 April ghost-restaurant ruling 
as another turn tighter in China Internet regulation and a simple EPS 
headwind for food delivery platforms. Our view is more constructive and 
more differentiated by stock. The direct fine is manageable and the 
operational remedy is narrow. The more important effect is economic: the 
ruling removes a pool of non-compliant, low-AOV, structurally loss-making 
orders, which should improve industry order quality and support higher AOV, 
revenue, and operating profit per order. On that basis, we see JD as the 
clearest positive read-through, Alibaba constructive via narrative de-risking, 
Meituan largely priced in, and PDD incrementally negative.
• What the market may be missing is that the affected volume is not high-
quality demand. Ghost restaurants were concentrated in delayed-delivery 
cake orders and skewed to low-ticket baskets that added volume but diluted 
unit economics. In our framework, removing that volume should reduce 
total orders modestly but improve the mix of orders that remain. That is 
why we read the ruling as supportive for industry profitability rather than as 
a broad demand shock. We also think the action matters beyond the one-off 
fine because it increases the probability of a more rational operating 
backdrop, even without assuming an explicit policy-led end to the subsidy 
war.
• The stock call therefore depends on what is already in numbers. For 
Alibaba, our model already embeds substantial food delivery and quick 
commerce loss narrowing, so the main benefit is lower narrative risk rather 
than incremental EPS upside versus consensus. For Meituan, the 2027 food 
delivery profit flip is already in our model and, we believe, largely in 
consensus. JD stands out because expectations remain low: our 2026E group 
earnings sit above consensus while our new initiatives loss path still assumes 
essentially no narrowing. Against that backdrop, even modest cost savings 
from cleaner order mix and potential subsidy rationalization matter more for 
the stock. PDD is the exception: the fine is absorbable, but the one-store-
one-penalty precedent adds a tail-risk overlay that we do not think the 
market fully prices.
• The main risks include: our thesis would weaken if ghost-restaurant 
exposure is smaller than we assume, if attrition of non-compliant low-AOV 
orders proves limited, or if platforms replace removed volume with equally 
uneconomic subsidized demand. The Alibaba de-risking mechanism also 
depends on management’s next public framing; if management reiterates 
aggressive investment without clearer discipline, the narrative benefit could 
fade. More broadly, if SAMR treats this as a one-off food-safety case rather 
than part of a wider push toward better order quality and more rational 
competition, the valuation read-through is likely to be more muted.

Bottom line
The investment question is not whether the RMB3.6bn aggregate fine 
matters to 2026 EPS. It is whether the 17 April SAMR ruling changes 
earnings expectations, valuation, or near-term positioning across the sector. 
We think the stock-relevant content lies in the interaction between what the 
ruling enables and what the market already discounts. On that basis, we 
rank the impact across our coverage as JD > Alibaba > Meituan, with 
PDD incrementally negative because of both the fine and the precedent 
set by the one-store-one-penalty template. In form, this is a one-time fine 
with narrow operational remedies. In economic substance, it improves 
industry order quality, narrows Alibaba’s credibility gap around a loss path 
that we believe is already in the stock, and establishes a reusable 
enforcement mechanism that raises tail risk at PDD.
JD is the clearest actionable expression of the ruling. On our estimates, the 
order-quality cleanup adds RMB0.7bn-1.6bn of annual run-rate cost 
savings as structurally loss-making sub-RMB30 orders exit the platforms. 
That more than covers roughly RMB300mn of recurring compliance cost 
and absorbs the RMB635mn fine within a single annual run-rate. More 
importantly, this positive increment sits on top of a pre-existing 
expectations gap: our 2026E group adjusted net income estimate is double-
digit percentage above consensus, while our new initiatives loss forecast of 
RMB46bn in 2025 and RMB45bn in 2026 still assumes essentially no 
narrowing and is consistent with where consensus sits. That is why we 
view JD as the cleanest single-stock way to express the ruling.
Alibaba is constructive, but the mechanism is narrative de-risking rather 
than incremental P&L upside versus consensus. Our food delivery and 
quick commerce loss forecasts of RMB88bn in FY26, RMB47bn in FY27, 
RMB23bn in FY28, and profitability in FY29 already imply an aggressive 
narrowing path. We believe the market broadly discounts a similar 
trajectory. The ruling does not, in our view, push P&L materially beyond 
that path. What it does is reduce the risk that management’s public 
language on aggressive investment leads investors to question whether the 
narrowing path will be followed. We see scope for a 5-10% equity re-

management’s next public framing shifting toward discipline, quality-led 
growth, and sustainable unit economics. We no longer view a 20% P&L-

the market.

The case matters because the economic substance is easy to miss if it is 
framed only as a generic food-safety action. The enforcement path began 
with a single consumer complaint in July 2025. A Beijing resident ordered 
a cake from a storefront called Tianyan Qingshu on a food delivery app, 
received a cake decorated with inedible and potentially toxic fresh roses, 
and filed a complaint. The local SAMR office could not find the store at its 
registered address, could not verify the uploaded food business license 
against the government database, and found that the listed supervising 
officer did not exist. The investigation then established that all 378 chain 
locations trading under the same brand name used forged licenses and 
operated without physical kitchens.
SAMR’s subsequent test-buy showed that the order had in fact been 
fulfilled by a different licensed bakery. The ghost storefront routed the 
order through Zhuandan Bao, a third-party order-transfer platform that 
operates a reverse auction, and the bakery fulfilled the order at the winning 
bid. That workflow, rather than a simple food-quality lapse, is the core of 
the case.

We think three features of this structure explain the severity of the 
response. First, the ghost storefront captured 48% of consumer spend for 
no production or delivery role, which supports SAMR’s view that this was 
a compliance-failure ecosystem rather than a pricing issue. Second, the 
actual producer was invisible to both the consumer and the platform’s 
compliance architecture, breaking the food-safety accountability chain that 
Article 62 of the Food Safety Law is designed to preserve. Third, Article 
18 of the Administrative Measures on Online Catering Service Food Safety 
explicitly prohibits order subcontracting by online food-service providers, 
which makes the transfer platforms, in the regulator’s framing, a 
mechanism to evade the law rather than a neutral intermediary.
Scale is what turned a statutory cap into a sector-level penalty event. 
SAMR identified 67,604 ghost storefronts across seven platforms and 
more than 3.6mn transferred cake orders, including 1.2mn+ through 
Zhuandan Bao and 2.4mn+ through Anhui Xunmeng, which we 
understand is PDD-affiliated. We think that, together with SAMR’s public 
reference to “violent and soft resistance to enforcement” during the 
investigation phase, helps explain why PDD absorbed RMB1.52bn, or 
42% of the aggregate fine.

The six weeks since Tencent's 4Q25 print have seen a repricing of China internet 
names along a different axis than the AI-narrative rally that carried the sector into

What Has Changed Since the 4Q25 Earnings Season
The 4Q25 reporting cycle that began on 18 March with Tencent's print did not produce large absolute earnings surprises, 
but it did reset the framework through which the market is underwriting China internet. In our view, six shifts matter.
Market regime inversion. Through late January 2026, Alibaba and Baidu rallied on an AI narrative despite weak 
fundamentals. After late January, profit-taking and a lower global risk appetite re-anchored the tape on near-term earnings 
and free cash flow. Most of the China internet names saw estimate cuts into and out of the 4Q print. Alibaba's US$100bn 
five-year cloud target, implying roughly a 47% cloud revenue 5-year CAGR did not support the stock. Long-dated narrative 
claims now carry a materially lower multiple than they did in 4Q25.
AI capex is being penalized, not rewarded. The US software analog rerating, IGV (iShares expanded Tech-Software 
Sector ETF) weakness and fears of disruption to core franchises have arrived in China with a lag. Linear extrapolations of 
AI spend (Tencent RMB36–40bn this year per the company), Alibaba's rapidly scaling of the "all others" line) produce ROI 
anxiety that the market is translating into multiple compression rather than multiple expansion. The burden of proof has 
shifted from "show us AI monetization is possible" to "show us AI monetization within the spend framework."
Regulatory wave re-opened, but bounded. The SAMR ghost-kitchen ruling landed on 18 April, with RMB35.97bn in 
aggregate industry penalties. PDD's RMB1.5bn fine is financially immaterial but carries signaling weight given unusually 
vivid domestic media coverage. A separate State Council-linked office probe into food delivery subsidy practices remains 
live. Trip.com's antitrust investigation, launched in early January, should be resolved on a four-to-six-month precedent 
timetable, implying May to July 2026. In our view, these are clearing events within an already-established framework, not a

Thematic Takeaways
The inference bottleneck is repricing the AI value chain in 
real time
The single most decision-useful observation from the Summit is that China’s AI 
profit pool is growing but currently captured upstream, by compute owners and 
hardware lessors, less by the model companies generating demand for that 
compute.
According to management of a leading domestic foundation model developer, the 
company had raised pricing on its latest-generation model by roughly double, yet 
margins improved only marginally because server rental costs rose sharply in a 
single recent month with no visible ceiling. A second model company reported that 
API and token-plan revenue has become the majority of total revenue, up from a 
minority share at year-end, with text-model API gross margins meaningfully above 
what it characterized as industry averages, driven by throughput efficiency rather 
than pricing. Despite this strong unit economics profile, the company’s ability to 
scale revenue remains constrained by inference compute availability, not demand.
We heard corroboration of this dynamic from the supply side. A global AI chip 
executive framed modern data centers as “token factories” where throughput 
converts directly to revenue and efficiency determines margins. His 
characterization of agentic AI systems as requiring “hundreds to thousands of 
times more compute” than earlier generative models reinforces the thesis that 
inference demand is likely to compound even as model architectures stabilize.
JPM view: the investable implication is that the market is pricing model-company 
equity narratives as if they are the primary beneficiaries of AI adoption, when in 
practice the economic surplus is flowing to compute infrastructure. This dynamic 
favors listed companies with vertically integrated chip-to-cloud stacks (Baidu via 
its proprietary AI chip subsidiary, Alibaba via T-Head/PPU) or platform 
incumbents that can internalize inference cost through proprietary hardware and 
ecosystem monetization (Tencent). Pure-play model companies (Zhipu AI, 
MiniMax) face the more precarious position of generating demand for compute 
without yet capturing proportional economics.
The compute supply chain has restructured, and the 
market has not caught up
According to management of multiple foundation model developers, they have 
shifted compute procurement away from major cloud service providers and toward 
specialized compute leasing companies, because the CSPs themselves are now 
constrained and competing for the same GPU resources they previously supplied. 
This is a structural change from last year, when CSPs were the primary 
procurement channel.

price of one specific server as having risen by roughly a third in the most recent 
month, on top of prior months of increases. The distinction between nominal 
global chip production capacity and actual cards reaching China was emphasized

internet products where incremental users improve economics, in AI each

SAMR’s draft food-delivery rules reduce Meituan’s downside tail, but they do not 
yet turn regulatory relief into a positive earnings catalyst, in our view. We 
reiterate Neutral on Meituan with a Dec-26 PT of HK$85, making no changes to 
our estimates considering the draft’s scenario framework. The call rests on one 
load-bearing assumption: SAMR’s funding-source language has real behavioral 
bite and limits capital-funded subsidy attacks on food delivery. If that premise 
holds, our reading of the draft lowers the probability of our bear case (Table 4), in 
which prolonged capital-funded competition weakens the delivery profit pool and 
Meituan’s fair value falls to around HK$51. If the premise does not hold, the rules 
become disclosure without much strategic value. We do not use the draft alone as 
the basis to change our estimates, but we see it as a near-term sentiment stabilizer 
ahead of the final text due after the July 17, 2026 consultation deadline, and 
considering 3Q26 subsidy evidence.
• The market appears to be pricing the absence of explicit subsidy caps as 
evidence that the draft lacks bite. In our view, that misreads the mechanism. The 
draft does not need to cap every campaign to matter; it needs to make the most 
aggressive campaigns slower, more visible, and easier to challenge. Seven-day 
pre-disclosure reduces surprise value, while funding-source reporting and 
competitive-impact self-assessment create an evidentiary trail for regulators, 
competitors, merchants, riders, and media to scrutinize extreme promotions.
• The relative stock implication favors Meituan on this specific catalyst, although 
not enough for us to prefer it outright over Alibaba (9988 HK/BABA US, OW) 
at the portfolio level. Meituan’s advantages lie in order density, routing 
efficiency, merchant coverage, rider management, and consumer habit 
formation. If the rules shift competition away from fast capital-funded cash 
subsidy toward operating execution, the battleground moves back onto 
Meituan’s terrain.

Why disclosure is enforcement
The first instinct is that “no caps” means “no bite.” We disagree.
The disclosure regime changes the economics of subsidy competition in three ways.
First, it slows the game down. Food-delivery subsidy wars are reactive. A challenger launches a promotion, the incumbent 
responds, and the market tests how far each side is willing to go. A seven-day public notice window reduces the value of 
surprise and weakens the tit-for-tat dynamic.
Second, it creates evidence. Funding-source disclosure and competitive-impact self-assessment force platforms to write 
down the facts that regulators would later need to evaluate whether a campaign relied on capital advantage or unfair 
competition.
Third, it raises the cost of aggression before penalties arrive. A campaign that must be disclosed in advance is easier for 
competitors, merchants, riders, media, and regulators to monitor. That reputational and enforcement risk should reduce the 
willingness to run extreme campaigns.
This is why we view the draft as more binding than it appears. It does not need to ban every subsidy to change platform 
behavior. It only needs to make the most aggressive campaigns harder to launch, justify, and repeat.

Industry implications
Competition: less cash burn, more operating execution
The rule reduces the value of capital-funded subsidy as a competitive shortcut. A challenger can still invest, but it becomes 
harder to disguise a share-buying campaign as ordinary promotion if the campaign must be disclosed in advance, justified 
publicly, and linked to a compliant funding source.
That matters because Meituan’s moat is operational rather than financial. Its advantage sits in order density, routing 
efficiency, merchant coverage, rider network management, and consumer habit formation. If the competitive game moves 
back toward these variables, Meituan’s relative position improves.
Operations: subsidy relief, but a higher cost floor
The draft should reduce the probability of extreme promotional escalation. At the same time, it reinforces a higher sector 
cost floor. Rules against forcing merchants or riders to bear subsidy costs, combined with rider social-insurance pressure, 
make it harder for platforms to offset promotion through cost shifting.

We retain our published industry profit-pool framework. In our base case, Meituan captures all or almost all positive food-

Stock implications
Meituan: the strategic beneficiary
Meituan is the stock most helped by the strategic logic of the draft. The rule reduces the probability of a prolonged capital-

The caveat is that Meituan’s own promotional toolkit is also constrained. It cannot rely on forced participation, below-cost

JD has the cleanest near-term earnings read-through. Its food-delivery entry has been subsidy-intensive, and the draft gives

JD’s food-delivery position becomes harder to underwrite.

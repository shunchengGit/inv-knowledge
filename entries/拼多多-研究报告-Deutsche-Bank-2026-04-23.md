Industry Update
 
Regulation returns – who benefits and 
who may lose out?
From price war to "compliance war"
The SAMR issued last Friday a series of administrative penalties totaling RMB3.6bn 
against major China e-commerce platforms, including PDD, Meituan, Alibaba, JD, 
and Douyin. These penalties cited the platforms' failure to filter out unqualified 
merchants and violations of food safety, particularly concerning "ghost kitchens" 
and "order transfer" practices on cakes. Under this model, platforms and ghost 
stores typically get 70% of the order price, leaving the actual fulfilling merchant very 
little to cover costs (Figure 2). Beyond financial penalties, operational restrictions 
like 3-9 month suspensions on onboarding new cake merchants were also 
imposed.
This action is notable for several key characteristics: 1) Record penalty over food 
safety. The total amount is the largest ever imposed since the 2015 amendment of 
China's Food Safety Law; 2) Broad scope. All major e-com platforms have been 
targeted, signaling an industry-wide compliance push, rather than isolated 
incidents; 3) Elevated individual accountability. RMB19.7m in fines were levied 
against platform legal representatives and food safety executives.
We view this, together with similar moves recently, as suggesting regulation is 
stepping up. It had been quiet since the anti-monopoly crackdown in 2021. In some 
ways this is the reverse of that move, as it appears an effort to reduce competition 
- but it does seem a valuable effort. Aggressive competition and low-quality supply 
will likely be targeted to reduce involution and elevate consumption. Meanwhile, 
regulators are explicitly requiring platforms to assume material responsibility for 
merchant oversight and consumer protection. This trend is also evident in the new 
tax rules for platforms effective last Oct, which mandate data-sharing between 
platforms and tax authorities to ensure merchant tax compliance. PDD was fined 
RMB100k for delaying tax data submission earlier this year.
While the penalties are substantial, they are not financially crippling for these deep-
pocketed giants. Even for PDD, the penalty only represents 1.4% of its 2025 non-
GAAP net income. However, we expect intensified regulatory pressure to create 
further near-term headwinds for industry growth and margins, as higher 
compliance costs incur and low-quality supply phase out. Over the long run, this 
should bring more positives, pushing the industry towards higher and more 
sustainable earnings however.

PDD TP cut by 5% to US$110, BABA by 2%
We believe the regulatory shift favours platforms with stronger quality positioning, 
higher proportion of branded merchants, and robust compliance capabilities, such 
as JD and Alibaba, while expect PDD to face a material negative impact. We update 
our estimates for the three companies factoring in the penalties and YTD trend. We 
cut our TP on PDD from US$115 to US$110, and retain a Hold. We also trim our TP 
on BABA to US$185 and HK$180, respectively on higher AI costs. Our TP for JD 
remains unchanged.
Among the three platforms, PDD has the largest exposure to small and unbranded 
merchants, many of whom relied on avoiding tax and qualification reviews to stay 
price-competitive. The company had the most active stores involved in the 
violations and received the highest penalty of RMB1.5bn, despite booking the 
lowest revenue. The severity of the fine was largely due to it reportedly supplying 
false information and even "adopting violence and soft resistance" to obstruct the 
investigation, according to SAMR and its affiliate China Quality Daily.

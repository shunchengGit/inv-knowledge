• We hosted JPM 22nd Global China Summit last week in Shanghai, conducting 
management meetings across platform incumbents, listed AI pure-plays, and 
consumer internet companies spanning our coverage universe. The observation 
that most reframes the China AI investment debate is that AI profit pool is 
expanding, but economic surplus is accruing upstream, to compute owners and 
specialized hardware lessors, not to the model companies generating demand 
for that infrastructure. Inference capacity, not training scale, is the binding 
constraint on industry revenue growth. The market prices model-layer 
companies as primary AI beneficiaries while the economics we observed 
suggest investors should assess upstream value capture leakage before 
underwriting margin expansion.
• The inference bottleneck has restructured compute procurement in ways not yet 
reflected in consensus models, in our view. Supply has migrated from major 
CSPs toward specialized leasing companies, where rental costs are rising. One 
model company almost doubled prices yet saw only marginal margin 
improvement as server costs absorbed the gain. Revenue growth at the model 
layer is supply-constrained, not demand-constrained, a distinction with direct 
earnings implications.
• Model convergence is compounding this pressure. Multiple management teams 
acknowledged that capability gaps among leading domestic models are 
narrowing, shifting competition from quality toward distribution and ecosystem 
integration — structurally constructive for platform incumbents, more 
challenging for pure-plays whose valuations embed durable quality premiums. 
Two distinct platform strategies have crystallized: Alibaba pursuing external 
cloud monetization, Tencent prioritizing internal ecosystem integration, 
implying divergent margin profiles the market cannot evaluate with a single AI-
revenue benchmark. Coding has emerged as the consensus monetization wedge, 
but subscription economics are already breaking down in favor of usage-based 
billing. No management team presented a convincing near-term path to mass 
consumer AI monetization, reinforcing a predominantly B2B revenue trajectory 
over the next 12–18 months.
• At the stock level, Tencent delivered the most significant AI narrative upgrade 
since 1Q26, with Hunyuan 3 adoption across 100-plus internal teams validating 
the ecosystem-first approach. Alibaba offered its clearest cloud margin 
disclosure to date, though macro commentary turned notably darker. Zhipu AI 
provided the sharpest illustration of upstream value capture, pricing power exists 
but surplus flows to lessors, ahead of the June 8 Stock Connect catalyst. 
MiniMax showed the strongest revenue acceleration but carries coding 
concentration risk and a July lock-up overhang. Baidu’s candid acknowledgment 
of foundation model momentum loss sharpens the full-stack thesis but 
introduces multi-quarter execution risk.

Thematic Takeaways
The inference bottleneck is repricing the AI value chain in 
real time
The single most decision-useful observation from the Summit is that China’s AI 
profit pool is growing but currently captured upstream, by compute owners and 
hardware lessors, less by the model companies generating demand for that 
compute.
According to management of a leading domestic foundation model developer, the 
company had raised pricing on its latest-generation model by roughly double, yet 
margins improved only marginally because server rental costs rose sharply in a 
single recent month with no visible ceiling. A second model company reported that 
API and token-plan revenue has become the majority of total revenue, up from a 
minority share at year-end, with text-model API gross margins meaningfully above 
what it characterized as industry averages, driven by throughput efficiency rather 
than pricing. Despite this strong unit economics profile, the company’s ability to 
scale revenue remains constrained by inference compute availability, not demand.
We heard corroboration of this dynamic from the supply side. A global AI chip 
executive framed modern data centers as “token factories” where throughput 
converts directly to revenue and efficiency determines margins. His 
characterization of agentic AI systems as requiring “hundreds to thousands of 
times more compute” than earlier generative models reinforces the thesis that 
inference demand is likely to compound even as model architectures stabilize.
JPM view: the investable implication is that the market is pricing model-company 
equity narratives as if they are the primary beneficiaries of AI adoption, when in 
practice the economic surplus is flowing to compute infrastructure. This dynamic 
favors listed companies with vertically integrated chip-to-cloud stacks (Baidu via 
its proprietary AI chip subsidiary, Alibaba via T-Head/PPU) or platform 
incumbents that can internalize inference cost through proprietary hardware and 
ecosystem monetization (Tencent). Pure-play model companies (Zhipu AI, 
MiniMax) face the more precarious position of generating demand for compute 
without yet capturing proportional economics.
The compute supply chain has restructured, and the 
market has not caught up
According to management of multiple foundation model developers, they have 
shifted compute procurement away from major cloud service providers and toward 
specialized compute leasing companies, because the CSPs themselves are now 
constrained and competing for the same GPU resources they previously supplied. 
This is a structural change from last year, when CSPs were the primary 
procurement channel.
Rental cost inflation is severe and accelerating. One company described the rental 
price of one specific server as having risen by roughly a third in the most recent 
month, on top of prior months of increases. The distinction between nominal 
global chip production capacity and actual cards reaching China was emphasized

Domestic chip alternatives remain a complement rather than a replacement. One 
company reported a domestic supplier offering only 50 cards in an entire month. 
The main domestic suppliers with meaningful inference volume were identified as

practical inference usability for their latest-generation chips generally estimated at

JPM view: model-company revenue growth over the next two to three quarters is 
likely constrained by compute availability more than by demand. Any easing of 
supply, whether through domestic chip ramp or rental price stabilization, would be 
a meaningful catalyst for the entire sector, benefiting Zhipu AI, MiniMax, and 
other model developers most directly. Conversely, continued rental inflation 
directly compresses the margin expansion case for model companies that lack 
proprietary chip supply. Cloud platforms that own proprietary chip capacity 
(Alibaba, Baidu) are relatively better positioned to absorb this cost pressure, 
though even they face constraints on available volume.
Domestic model convergence shifts competition from 
capability to distribution
According to management of multiple foundation model developers, the domestic 
competitive landscape has no generational capability gap among leading players. 
One described the market as not winner-take-all, with enterprise users combining 
models based on task-specific features (speed, long-context performance, 
multimodal understanding, reasoning difficulty) rather than consolidating around a 
single provider. A second company argued that benchmark leadership has become 
“easily optimized” and the meaningful test is whether paying customers choose the 
model in production workflows. A senior technology panel participant reinforced 
this from a product perspective, arguing that platform integration, user context 
accumulation, and workflow lock-in are becoming the durable moats.
This convergence thesis has asymmetric implications across the coverage universe. 
For platform incumbents, it is constructive: if the model layer is approaching 
“good enough” quality, then distribution, ecosystem, and data advantages become 
the primary competitive differentiators. One major platform demonstrated this 
operationally, describing how a rebuilt AI team delivered a competitive model 
within 88 days of full team readiness, which was then integrated across more than 
100 internal product teams within weeks.
For pure-play model companies at high ARR multiples, the convergence thesis is 
more challenging. One such company defended premium pricing by arguing that at 
close to a leading US lab’s capability level, discounting makes no sense because 
customers will simply pay slightly more for the genuine article. This logic depends 
on a quality gap that the broader summit evidence suggests is narrowing. We note, 
however, that this company’s own customer feedback and retention data were 
described as strong. The tension between “we are the best” self-assessments and 
the broader “no generational gap” market structure observation is itself an 
important analytical signal, and the resolution likely determines the direction of AI 
pure-play valuations over the next 6–12 months.

JPM view: model convergence is structurally constructive for Tencent 
(distribution via WeChat, mini-programs, payments) and Alibaba (cloud platform, 
merchant ecosystem, payment rails). It is more threatening for Zhipu AI and 
MiniMax, whose valuations depend on durable quality premiums. Baidu sits in 
between: its model capability has lagged recently, but its proprietary AI chip

and value migrates to infrastructure.
Coding is the consensus monetization wedge, but 
subscription economics are failing
Across foundation model companies, cloud platforms, and internet incumbents, 
coding emerged as the vertical where AI revenue is actually materializing at scale. 
One company reported that the vast majority of token consumption is coding-
related. Another described coding as its priority weakness and the area requiring 
the most urgent improvement. A third platform claimed its coding product leads 
domestic DAU rankings in the category. A Chinese AI executive positioned 
coding as the fastest area for top domestic models to catch up with US labs.
